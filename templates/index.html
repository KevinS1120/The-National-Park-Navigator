Critical Issues Facing National Parks
Overcrowding and Visitor Impact
(John Burcham|The New York Times) Cars line up at the South Rim entrance of Grand Canyon National Park in Arizona on July 2, 2021.
Visitation Growth
According to the National Park Service, national park visitation grew from 285,891,275 in the year 2000 to 325,498,646 in 2023, an increase of roughly 14% (National Park Service, 2024).
(National Park Service) Yearly visitation numbers for all parks over the past four years.
Increased numbers of vehicles, use of public facilities, litter, wear and tear on hiking trails, and an overall increase in congestion within parks are all direct impacts caused by this uptick in visitation.
Combating the Issue
In order to combat the negative impacts caused by the growing number of people visiting these parks, many strategies have been put into place.
(NPS/Abi Farish) The electric shuttles of Zion national park help the NPS work towards sustainability goals and reduce overcrowding within the park.
Different means of transportation, such as electric shuttles are being used in parks such as Zion National Park in Utah, backcountry passes are required for those desiring to camp and hike in the backcountry of most national parks, timed entry systems are being utilized at several parks, such as Arches National Park and Muir Woods National Monument, and vehicle reservations are all being utilized to great effect in an attempt to combat overcrowding (Bradybaugh, 2022).
(National Park Service) The timed entry system at Arches National Park helps to combat overcrowding.
While these measures have helped manage the increasing number of visitors, they also highlight the need for continued efforts in sustainable park management. As visitation numbers continue to rise, finding a balance between public enjoyment and environmental protection will be crucial for preserving the future of these landscapes.
Climate Change
Global Warming
(National Park Service) Glacier National Park's Jackson Glacier, pictured in 1910, left, and 2020.
As time passes and the planet warms, climate change has an increasingly unpredictable impact on public lands in the U.S.. A premier example of this can be seen in Glacier National Park. Known for its remarkable glaciers, Glacier National Park is a remarkable example of the impact of climate change on our public lands. As temperatures steadily rise around the world, this park has not been spared from the effects. Throughout the years, the glaciers that give this magnificent park its name have been melting at an increasingly rapid rate, and the largest glaciers in the park have lost upwards of 75% of their size since the mid 1800s (National Park Service).
Flooding and Wildfire
(National Park Service) Yellowstone's North Entrance Road was washed out by flooding.
Even some of the most-visited national parks in the world can see devastation due to climate change, and Yellowstone National Park was no exception to this phenomenon. Yellowstone experienced unprecedented amounts of rainfall, which led to historically high flooding and water levels. Not only does this sort of natural disaster cause millions of dollars in damages, but it causes irreparable damage to the surrounding ecosystem as well (National Park Service, 2023).
(Knoxville Mercury) Deadly wildfire in Tennessee, 2016.
Wildfires in public lands bring with them destruction due to their devastating heat and uncontrollable blazes. Ecologically, fire is an integral part of many different ecosystems. In the Great Smoky Mountains National Park, certain types of pine trees depend on fires to open their sealed cones and disperse seeds over fire-cleared ground, and federally-endangered Red-cockaded Woodpeckers only nest in pine trees free of surrounding underbrush that is oftentimes cleared by fire (National Park Service, 2021). Despite the benefits that come with fire, fire suppression practices over the past 60-70 years, coupled with construction of residential areas near the park boundary have created issues within the park. When the natural occurrence of fires is suppressed, it allows an increased buildup of underbrush that increases the odds for devastating wildfires due to the increased material to burn (National Park Service, 2021). ‚ÄúIn 2015, a wildfire killed 14 people, damaged or destroyed more than 2,500 homes, and burned 11,000 acres of Great Smoky Mountains National Park, getting within 2,000 feet of a major visitor center and forcing the evacuation of 14,000 residents‚Äù (NPCA, 2025). The Ferguson Fire of 2019 and the Tim Fire of 2013 both had incredibly harmful effects in California, and combined burned tens of thousands of acres of protected lands within Yosemite National Park (NPCA, 2025).
Risk to Wildlife
(NPS/Tim Lambert) The presence of brook trout indicates clean, cold water.
The effects of global warming also causes great harm to wildlife within our national parks. ‚ÄúSome biologists estimate that 35% of animals and plants could become extinct in the wild by 2050 due to global climate change‚Äù (National Park Service, 2025). Heating waters put numerous different species of wildlife at risk in places such as Point Reyes National Seashore in California, including but not limited to sea otters, short-tailed albatross, steller sea lion, loggerhead sea turtle, and gray whales, just to name a few (National Park Service, 2025). In Shenandoah National Park, studies on brook trout showed similarly worrying results. Due to warming waters, results over a span of 27 years of data collection show that brook trout populations declined by ‚Äúat least 50 percent in more than 70 percent of Shenandoah‚Äôs streams‚Äù (Wofford, Childress, 2024). In lower-elevation streams where water warms quicker, trout populations were increasingly devastated and populations disappeared completely in at least three streams (Wofford, Childress, 2024).
Government Legislature
History
(NPS) Theodore Roosevelt.
Theodore Roosevelt, often referred to as the ‚Äúconservation president,‚Äù spearheaded the creation of national parks, monuments, and wildlife refuges to ensure their protection. Roosevelt felt that the decimation of species native to these lands was a loss that underlines the way society perceives our natural resources (National Park Service, 2017).
(NPS) Theodore Roosevelt and John Muir at Yellowstone National Park.
During his presidency, Roosevelt enacted the Antiquities Act on June 8th, 1906, which grants the president the authority to provide general legal protection of cultural and natural resources of historic or scientific interest (National Park Service, 2025). Through the Antiquities Act, Roosevelt established five new national parks, four national monuments, greatly expanded the protections of areas surrounding the Grand Canyon as well as placing protections on six predominantly cultural areas and twelve natural areas. Following in the footsteps of Theodore Roosevelt, under the unusually long term of Franklin Delano Roosevelt (FDR) from 1933 to 1945, FDR established the United States Forest Service, 150 national forests, 51 federal bird reserves, 4 national game preserves, 5 national parks, and 18 national monuments through the Antiquities Act, totalling approximately 230 million acres of public land (National Park Service, 2020). Despite the importance that historical figures placed on the protection of public lands, recent policies have threatened these very protections.
Shrinking Protections
(Southern Utah Wilderness Alliance) The shrinking of Bears Ears National Monument.
The current president of the U.S. has the power to change the way that public lands are allocated, and are therefore able to shrink, expand, and otherwise alter these lands. This was seen during the first term of Donald Trump‚Äôs presidency, during which roughly half of the Bears Ears and Grand Staircase-Escalante National Monuments were opened to the harvesting of resources (NRDC, 2024).
Federal Hiring Freeze
(The White House) The federal hiring freeze of 2025.
On January 20th, Trump signed an executive order which states ‚ÄúAs part of this freeze, no Federal civilian position that is vacant at noon on January 20, 2025, may be filled, and no new position may be created except as otherwise provided for in this memorandum or other applicable law‚Äù (The White House, 2025). Not only does this hiring freeze impact white-collar positions within the government, this also impacts National Park Service employees ranging from janitors to park rangers and law-enforcement staff. This means that at this time no additional staff in any of these groups can be hired, and in the event that someone leaves their position, they cannot even be replaced under the conditions of this executive order.
Resource Extraction
(Steve Keller/Dreamstime.com) Colorado oil pump.
Between 1911 and 1938, more than $200 million worth of copper was extracted from the Kennecott Copper Mine, now a historic protected location within Wrangell-St. Elias National Park in Alaska (National Park Service). With this sort of economic value, it is easy to see the reason that many push for the allowance of resource extraction in these protected areas. A contemporary example of this is the ongoing efforts by the Utah state legislature to shrink the protections provided to their resident national parks, as the legislature wishes to open parts of these lands to resource extraction and uses other than those allowed by these protections (Leshy, 2023).
Invasive Species
Invasive Species
Invasive species pose a substantial threat to plants and animals, and those within our public lands are no exception. Native species are species that are found in an ecosystem naturally, not having been brought to the area or distributed by humans. Invasive species are non-native, and their introduction to a foreign ecosystem causes economic or environmental harm (National Park Service, 2024). Invasive species can cause harm to ecosystems in many ways, including direct competition for food with native species, predation on native species, and even causing decline in native populations through feeding habits. Additionally, the rapid rate at which invasive species tend to reproduce lead to a tendency of taking over areas shortly after their introduction.
Asian Carp
(USGS) Silver carp can jump when startled, possibly causing damage or injury to boaters.
Asian carp are an example of a prevalent invasive species causing destruction in the waterways of our public lands. Asian carp are a broad term for four types of invasive fish species: bighead carp (Hypophthalmichthys nobilis), black carp (Mylopharyngodon piceus), grass carp (Ctenopharyngodon idella), and silver carp (Hypophthalmichthys molitrix) (U.S. Department of Agriculture). These carp cause harm to the environment due to their predation on and competition with native fish species for food and habitat (U.S. Department of the Interior, 2016). One concern regarding the spread of Asian carp across waterways is the potential for their establishment in the Great Lakes region of the Northeastern United States. While scientists debate the impact of their introduction in these lakes, it would likely have a devastating effect based on what they have done in other waterways. Asian carp tend to become direct competitors to native species for food, as they require large quantities of food due to their large size; consume smaller, vulnerable species such as endangered species of mussel, putting them in further peril; and bring with them an overall decrease in water quality due to their foraging habits (‚ÄúAsian Carp and the Great Lakes Region‚Äù).
(Peter Hermes Furian/Adobe Stock) The Great Lakes.
In addition, severe economic impact is expected: ‚Äúit has been widely reported that Great Lakes fisheries generate U.S. economic activity of approximately $7 billion annually‚Äù (‚ÄúAsian Carp and the Great Lakes Region‚Äù). Additionally, millions of people fish the Great Lakes every year, which brings in thousands of job opportunities and billions of dollars. If Asian carp are to find their way into the Great Lakes system, there is the potential to lose billions of dollars in economic activity due to the impact of these fish (‚ÄúAsian Carp and the Great Lakes Region‚Äù).
Plants
(NPS) Tamarisk slightly impaired by the tamarisk leaf beetle
The United States National Park Service approximates that 1.4 million acres of protected lands and waters are covered by invasive plant life (National Park Service, 2022). Invasive plants can disrupt ecosystems by outcompeting native species for resources and space, often leading to changes in the composition and health of plant life in the area. In Grand Canyon National Park, tamarisk (Tamarix) was introduced in the late 1920s to combat erosion and stabilize riverbanks. The unintended consequence of this introduction was its rapid spread and domination of the environment, crowding out native plants. Tamarisk can reach 15 to 50 feet tall, and a mature plant can use up to 200 gallons of water per day (Callaghan, 2025). Tamarisk seeds are very low in nutrients, meaning they also don‚Äôt provide much sustenance for birds and other animals in the park (Callaghan, 2025).
